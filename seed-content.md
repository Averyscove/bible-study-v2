# v2 launch — group seed content

> **Status: DRAFT for Neptune's review.** Nothing in this file has been written to the database. Once approved, this content goes into Firestore as the initial state of v2's 6 launch groups, owned by a "Bible Study Team" account.

The voice across all six groups aims to match the v1 app's Reading Companion: thoughtful, intentional, slightly literary, never preachy. Welcoming to newcomers, not condescending to mature readers, honest about the hard parts.

Each group ships with:

- A short tagline (shown in the group list)
- A longer description (shown at the top of the group page)
- Book and level tags
- A pinned welcome post in the **Discussions** tab
- 2 starter conversation prompts in the **Discussions** tab
- An opening message in the **Chat** tab

The Bible quotes throughout use the World English Bible (public domain), matching v1.

## Group 1 — Meeting Jesus: Gospel of John

**Slug:** `john`
**Focus books:** John
**Level:** First time / All welcome
**Visibility:** Public

### Tagline (group list)

We're reading John together — the gospel that introduces Jesus in his own words. Beginner-friendly. Bring your questions.

### Description (group page header)

The Gospel of John was written so you'd know who Jesus is. Not "a great teacher," not "a religious figure" — the Word made flesh, the Son of God, the one through whom everything was made. Whether this is your first time reading the Bible or your fiftieth, this is where the journey begins. Take it slow. Ask the real questions. We're glad you're here.

### Pinned welcome post (Discussions)

Welcome.

Why John first? Because everything in the Christian faith hinges on who Jesus is, and John writes so you can see him clearly. He doesn't open with a genealogy or a birth story — he opens with cosmology. "In the beginning was the Word." He wants you to know, from line one, that you're meeting someone who was there before there was a "before."

A few things about this group:

- Don't worry if questions come up. That's the point.
- Half-formed thoughts are welcome. You don't have to have it figured out to share.
- If something doesn't make sense or sits wrong with you, say so. Someone else is probably wondering the same thing.
- We're not here to win arguments. We're here to read together.

If you're new to the Bible: take it one chapter at a time. The reading plan in the app will pace you.

If you've been doing this a while: you've read John before, but you haven't read it with these specific people. Bring your wisdom. Bring your honest doubts too — the people newer than you need both.

First conversation starter is below. Jump in any time.

### Starter conversation 1 (Discussions)

John 1 opens with: "In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God."

What did you make of that on your first read? What didn't make sense?

No wrong answers. Confused is a perfectly fine starting place.

### Starter conversation 2 (Discussions)

John keeps using the word "believe" — over 90 times in 21 chapters. More than any other gospel. He's clearly trying to do something with that word.

What do you think it means to "believe in Jesus"? Just agreeing he existed? Trusting him with something? Something else?

### Opening chat message

Glad you're here. The chat moves a little faster than the discussions — drop a quick thought, say which chapter you're on, ask a quick question. We're slow Bible readers but quick listeners.

## Group 2 — Romans: Why Jesus Matters

**Slug:** `romans`
**Focus books:** Romans
**Level:** Going deeper
**Visibility:** Public

### Tagline

After meeting Jesus in John, Romans tells you what his life and death actually accomplished. The gospel, explained.

### Description

Paul wrote Romans for people who'd never met Jesus and needed everything explained: why we're broken, what God did about it, how that changes life from the inside out. It's the most carefully argued book in the New Testament — and the one that has changed more lives than maybe any other. We read it slowly because it deserves to be read slowly.

### Pinned welcome post

Romans is a heavy book. Not depressing-heavy — substantial. You'll feel that within the first chapter.

Paul is writing to Christians in Rome he's never met. He wants them to understand the gospel deeply enough that they don't get pushed around by every wind that blows through. So he starts at the bottom — humanity is broken, all of us, religious and irreligious alike. Then he builds: God made a way through Jesus. That way is received by faith, not earned. And once you've received it, your whole life changes shape.

This group is for people who've spent time with Jesus already (probably John, probably Luke) and now want to wrestle with what it all *means*. Bring your hard questions. Some chapters in this book have divided the church for two thousand years; we won't solve those debates, but we can read with each other honestly.

A few notes:

- This book rewards re-reading. Don't be afraid to circle back.
- Some passages will be hard the first time. That's the book, not you.
- Disagreements are welcome. Personal attacks aren't. Group leader will step in if it gets there.

Starter conversation below.

### Starter conversation 1

Romans 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."

How does that statement land for you? Heavy? True? Both? Something you've made peace with, or something you still bristle at?

No need to say the "right" thing. Honest is more useful here.

### Starter conversation 2

Paul keeps coming back to the word "righteousness" — God's righteousness, our righteousness, righteousness through faith.

If a friend asked you what "righteousness" means in this book, how would you explain it in your own words?

### Opening chat message

Welcome. This chat is for the small thoughts — the verse that hit you this morning, the question that's bugging you. The discussion tab is where the bigger conversations live. Whichever feels right, jump in.

## Group 3 — Following Jesus through Luke

**Slug:** `luke`
**Focus books:** Luke
**Level:** All welcome
**Visibility:** Public

### Tagline

Luke gives us the most thorough account of Jesus's life — birth, teaching, miracles, death, resurrection. We read for the stories that show who he really is.

### Description

Luke was a doctor. He pays attention. His gospel is the longest book in the New Testament because he didn't want to leave anything out — the people Jesus met, the words he spoke, the meals he shared, the way he died. Read Luke and you watch Jesus move through a real world, with real people, in real time. Watch who he chooses. Watch how he loves.

### Pinned welcome post

Luke begins with one of the most quietly remarkable lines in the Bible: "Many have undertaken to draw up an account of the things that have been fulfilled among us… With this in mind, since I myself have carefully investigated everything from the beginning, I too decided to write an orderly account."

This is a doctor doing journalism. Luke isn't passing along legend — he's compiling testimony. Eyewitnesses, primary sources. He wanted you to be able to trust what you're reading.

What you'll find: the longest, most detailed narrative of Jesus's life in the Bible. The birth stories you think you know but probably don't. Parables that have shaped Western literature. Encounters with women, foreigners, sinners, religious leaders, and a thief on a cross. Death. Resurrection. A meal at Emmaus.

This group is for anyone reading Luke at any pace, any level. The chapters can feel long; the stories are worth slowing down for.

Starter conversation below.

### Starter conversation 1

Luke includes stories the other gospels skip — the prodigal son, the good Samaritan, Zacchaeus in the tree, the thief on the cross.

Which story in Luke has stayed with you the most? Why? (And if you haven't read enough yet to answer, no rush — come back when you have one.)

### Starter conversation 2

Watch how Jesus eats in this gospel. He eats with sinners, with Pharisees, with the disciples, with strangers on the road to Emmaus. The meals matter to Luke.

What do you make of how often Jesus is at the table?

### Opening chat message

Welcome. Luke is long — 24 chapters. The pace here matches that. Slow, story-loving, no rush. Drop a thought when you have one.

## Group 4 — Acts: The Church Born

**Slug:** `acts`
**Focus books:** Acts
**Level:** All welcome
**Visibility:** Public

### Tagline

What happened after Jesus rose? Twelve scared men become a movement that crosses the Roman Empire. Acts is the story of the church beginning.

### Description

Acts is sometimes called "The Acts of the Apostles," but really it's the Acts of the Holy Spirit. Luke (the same author as the gospel) wrote it as a sequel — Jesus rises, leaves, and the Spirit comes. From there, ordinary people change the world. We read Acts to see what the Spirit looks like in motion: bold preaching, unexpected conversions, prison breaks, shipwrecks, and a gospel that crosses every border anyone tries to put on it.

### Pinned welcome post

Acts opens with the disciples staring up at the sky after Jesus ascends. They're confused. They're afraid. Two angels show up and basically say, "Why are you standing here looking up? Get to work."

What follows is one of the most thrilling books in the Bible. The Spirit shows up at Pentecost. Peter — the same Peter who denied Jesus three times — preaches in front of thousands and 3,000 people respond. The church is born.

Then it gets harder. Persecution. Stephen is killed. Saul is hunting Christians. And then, on a road to Damascus, Saul meets the risen Jesus and everything changes — he becomes Paul, and the rest of Acts traces him as he carries the gospel further and further out, from Jerusalem to Rome.

This group is for anyone reading Acts at any level. It's a long book (28 chapters) but it reads like an adventure novel. Take your time.

Starter conversation below.

### Starter conversation 1

Acts 2 — Pentecost. The Spirit shows up in fire and sound. People hear the gospel in their own languages.

What does that scene tell you about how God works? Why fire? Why languages? What do you make of it?

### Starter conversation 2

Saul's conversion in Acts 9 is one of the most dramatic moments in the book — the most aggressive enemy of the church becomes its biggest champion.

Have you ever known someone whose life turned around like that? What did it look like?

### Opening chat message

Welcome. Acts moves fast — feel free to do the same here. Quick reactions, "I'm on chapter X and…" Drop a thought any time.

## Group 5 — Old Testament Foundations

**Slug:** `ot-foundations`
**Focus books:** Genesis, Exodus, Psalms, Proverbs, Isaiah
**Level:** Serious study
**Visibility:** Public

### Tagline

After meeting Jesus, the Old Testament looks different. Selected passages — creation, Abraham, the Passover, key Psalms, the suffering servant. The shape of the whole story.

### Description

The Old Testament is long, and at first glance can feel distant. But once you've spent time with Jesus, the whole thing reads forward toward him. The reading plan picks out the most important moments — creation, the fall, Abraham, the Exodus, the Ten Commandments, key Psalms, the prophet Isaiah's vision of a suffering servant. Each one is a piece of the picture Jesus completes. We read these together to see the larger story we've been part of all along.

### Pinned welcome post

If you're here, you've probably been through the New Testament already. Welcome back to the beginning.

The Old Testament was the only Bible Jesus had. He read these scrolls. He quoted them constantly. When he said "the Scriptures testify about me," these are the Scriptures he meant. So we read them now — not as a dry history book, but as the part of the story that has been pointing the whole way.

The plan picks out 13 passages: creation and the fall, Abraham, the Passover, the Ten Commandments, three Psalms, two chapters of Proverbs, the suffering servant in Isaiah. Each one is foundational. Each one matters.

This group moves more slowly than the New Testament groups. The text is older. The world it describes is stranger. We sit with it longer.

Starter conversation below.

### Starter conversation 1

Read Genesis 1–3 slowly. Don't rush.

What's God like in those chapters? What's humanity like? What changes between chapter 2 and chapter 3?

### Starter conversation 2

Isaiah 52–53 was written about 700 years before Jesus. Read it and notice what it describes — a servant who suffers, who is rejected, who carries the sins of others.

When you read it now, what do you see? What questions does it raise?

### Opening chat message

Welcome. The OT is older and stranger than what you've been reading. Slow is the right speed here. Drop in whenever.

## Group 6 — Memory Verses: Hidden Words

**Slug:** `memory`
**Focus books:** (cross-cutting)
**Level:** All welcome
**Visibility:** Public

### Tagline

Eight verses worth knowing by heart. We practice them, share what they're doing in us, and encourage each other to keep going.

### Description

Memorizing scripture is a slow, quiet work. Some days you'll feel sharp; some days you'll forget the verse you just learned. The eight verses we're practicing here are foundational — when you know them, the rest of the Bible reads differently. They show up in your mind during prayers, during arguments, during the hard moments. We're learning them together because doing it alone is harder.

### Pinned welcome post

The eight verses in this practice are not random. They're the ones the New Testament keeps coming back to — the ones that, if you know them, give you a frame for almost everything else you read.

John 3:16. John 14:6. Romans 3:23. Romans 5:8. Romans 6:23. Romans 8:1. Ephesians 2:8–9. Psalm 23:1.

Read those references again. They're the gospel in compressed form.

Memorizing them isn't about being a "good Christian." It's about furnishing the inside of your mind with words that hold up. When you're awake at 3am and your thoughts are loud, having "There is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" already in your head is different from having to look it up.

A few notes about this group:

- Forgetting is normal. It's part of the work.
- Share what's hard. The verse you can't seem to lock in is the one most worth talking about.
- The practice mode in the app uses gradual word-hiding. Use it. It works.

Starter conversation below.

### Starter conversation 1

Which of the eight verses are you working on right now? What's making it stick — or what's making it hard?

### Starter conversation 2

If you know all eight already: which one has shown up in your mind the most over the years? When? What was happening?

### Opening chat message

Welcome. This space is for short check-ins, encouragement, and the moments when a verse you've been working on suddenly clicks. Drop in any time.
